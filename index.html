Big Bang Nucleosynthesis as a Probe of New Physics

Image credit - Astronomy: Roen Kelly

Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) provides us with a window into the very early stages of the Universe, when it was only about 1 second old. How is that possible? Around that time, many of the revelant processes happen to proceed almost out-of-equilibrium, such that even small deviations in the primordial history can lead to big changes in the observed values of the primordial abundances. Given the marvelous agreement between the measured and predicted abundances of helium-4 and deuterium within the Standard Model, BBN has proven to be an important checkpoint in cosmology. And this, of course, has interesting consequences for new physics models of all kinds.

For example, it can be used to constrain thermal dark sectors (1910.01649; see also 2107.11232 for a re-analysis with the updated LUNA rates), deviations in the gravitational constant (1910.10730), and Heavy Neutral Leptons (2006.07387). In the last paper, something interesting is observed: HNLs can decrease the effective numner of relativistic species in the early Universe even if they decay mostly into neutrinos. An explanation of this effect - which happens with any FIMP that decays into neutrinos with high enough energies - is provided in 2103.09831.

How Dwarf Spheroidal Galaxies Can Constrain Fermionic Dark Matter

Image credit - ESO/Digitized Sky Survey 2

What if it turns out that dark matter is simply non-interacting? Is all hope lost then? Well, if it is fermionic, then we still might be able to say something about its mass. A possible way in doing so involves determining the phase-space density of a gravitationally bound object and comparing that to the theoretically predicted value. As it turns out, dwarf spheroidal galaxies are perfect systems for this purpose, mainly because of their large central densities and low velocity dispersions.

In a recent work (2010.03572), we utilised a state-of-the-art Jeans analysis to obtain the most stringent bounds on fermionic dark matter with this method to date.

Measuring the Clustering of Matter with High-Redshift Galaxies

There is still a lot unknown about the conditions of the Universe at high redshifts and small scales. UV galaxy luminosity functions (UV LFs) can provide us with some insightful information in this regards. Indeed, UV LFs tell us something about the abundance of young galaxies in the Universe, which means that they track the evolution of the mass function of dark matter halos, both at earlier times and lower masses than local Universe probes. We are talking here about redshifts 4 to 10-ish, an epoch we don't really know that much about and which makes it an interesting regime to constrain, for example, the nature of dark matter and primordial fluctuations at small scales. There is already some good quality data available from the Hubble Space Telescope, and upcoming telescopes such as the James Webb Space Telescope and Roman Space Telescope, could further improve our current understanding. This of course motivates us to do some exciting physics with this probe!

We tested the grounds with this probe by looking how it can constrain small-scale non-Gaussianities (2009.01245). The word "small-scale" here indicates scales that are smaller than can be probed by Cosmic Microwave Background observations. Interestingly, with current data we can already constrain fNL to be consistent with zero within a few hundred.

A follow-up paper is in the works. Stay tuned...
